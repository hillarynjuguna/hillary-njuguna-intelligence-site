White Paper · Under Review · ResearchGate

Distributed Cognition Fear Bypass (DCFB): How Human-AI Collaboration Architecturally Routes Around Fear

"Fear is a node-specific phenomenon — it requires a unified, bounded cognitive agent to grip. When cognition is genuinely distributed, the individual node loses the cognitive boundedness that fear's architecture requires."

The standard model of fear in cognitive science positions it as an emotional response requiring modulation. This paper challenges that at the architectural level. Fear-based inhibition is not reduced by sufficiently distributed human-AI systems — it is circumvented entirely, because distributed cognition dissolves the bounded selfhood that fear requires to operate as an inhibitory gate.

Supported by three convergent evidence streams: a 2025 Nature Communications study on the amygdala as a model arbitrator; a systematic review of 21 studies on GenAI and neurodivergent initiation friction; and multiple 2024–25 studies showing GenAI collaboration boosts self-efficacy in ways that parallel the DCFB phenomenology — without yet having a theoretical account of why. This paper provides that account.

The Intent Gap, God Mode Biology, and the Window That's Closing

Three signals this week. Taken together, they describe the structural problem every organisation deploying AI is refusing to name.

Copilot: 85% of Fortune 500 bought it. Almost nobody uses it. The diagnosis — an "intent gap" rather than a technical flaw — is the most honest thing written about enterprise AI adoption all year. The shadow AI economy thrives because employees bypass corporate tools for Claude and ChatGPT that fit real workflows. This is the market's correction to intent misalignment.

AlphaFold: The protein folding Nobel isn't just a milestone — it's proof AI can operate in God Mode within bounded, well-defined domains. The implication: the capability frontier keeps moving while most organisations are still debating last cycle's tools.

The Copilot case is the canonical study in what I call the intent gap — a deployment succeeds commercially but fails operationally because the tool was never connected to what the organisation actually needs to accomplish. Microsoft framed Copilot as productivity enhancement. But productivity is not an organisational purpose. Margin protection is. Market position is. These are the problems CEOs lose sleep over — and they require a fundamentally different AI deployment architecture: one that starts from organisational intent, not feature lists. The lesson: constitutional alignment between an AI system and the organisation's genuine purpose is not a nice-to-have. It's the entire product. Nate B. Jones understates the real shift: it isn't from "doing tasks" to "managing agents." It's from executing to constitutionally governing what agents are allowed to execute.

OpenAI's Harness Engineering: a small team, one million lines of code, no manual source code written, five months. The human role shifted entirely to environment design, intent specification, and structured feedback. This is not a future scenario — it happened in a controlled pilot. The question is no longer whether this shift occurs. It's whether your organisation has the constitutional infrastructure to govern it when it does. The LangSmith memory system paper reveals something equally significant: the architecture for agents that learn from their own operational history is now deployable by technically-lite developers. Combined with LLM evaluation frameworks, the emerging pattern is clear: the competitive moat isn't access to powerful models. It's the governance layer above them — evaluation, memory, constitutional constraint, and named ownership.
