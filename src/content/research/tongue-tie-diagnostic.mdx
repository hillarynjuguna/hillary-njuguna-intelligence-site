Can your organization articulate, in writing, what its AI systems are optimizing for?

That question appears in the homepage assessment widget. The three responses -- yes, no, partially -- map onto a diagnostic that the Martha framework calls the Tongue-Tie Diagnostic. The name is clinical, not metaphorical. Tongue-tie is the physical condition where the tongue cannot achieve full range of motion because it is structurally constrained. The articulable range of motion is limited not by what the person wants to say but by what the underlying architecture permits.

The organizational version is analogous. An organization with a well-articulated intent specification can describe what its AI systems are doing. An organization with tongue-tie can operate those systems, direct them, expand them, and discuss their outputs -- but cannot articulate the constitutional layer: what they are optimizing for, under what conditions they should stop, whose authority defines the stopping condition.

---

## The Diagnostic Structure

The Tongue-Tie Diagnostic has four questions. They are asked in sequence, each narrowing the target:

**Question 1: Can you describe what the system does?**
This is the operational layer. Most organizations pass. The system processes data, generates outputs, routes requests, classifies inputs. This description is available because it is the layer being directly observed.

**Question 2: Can you describe what the system is optimizing for?**
This is the intent specification layer. Fewer organizations pass. The system is optimizing for engagement, or accuracy, or efficiency -- but when pressed: accuracy on what measure, under what conditions, with what failure tolerance? The specification gets thinner under pressure.

**Question 3: Can you describe the conditions under which the system should stop?**
This is the constitutional layer. The stopping conditions. The kill signals. The R3 threshold. Most organizations do not have explicit answers to this question. The system stops when someone decides to stop it. The decision authority is unspecified. The conditions are implicit.

**Question 4: Who is accountable if those conditions are crossed without authorization?**
This is the governance constitution. Named authority. Explicit accountability. The moment this question produces a silence, a shrug, or a redirect to policy documentation, the diagnostic is complete. The organization has tongue-tie.

---

## Why This is an Architecture Problem

The standard response to tongue-tie is communication improvement. Better documentation. Clearer policy language. More training. These interventions address the articulable layer but not the constitutional layer. An organization can produce excellent documentation of a system whose intent specification, stopping conditions, and accountability architecture do not exist in practice.

The Tongue-Tie Diagnostic is looking for something different from documentation quality. It is looking for whether the constitutional layer exists -- not in policy documents, but in the operational reality of the organization. If the system has never actually stopped because a stopping condition was triggered, the stopping conditions exist only in the specification layer. If no one has ever invoked the accountability structure in response to an AI governance failure, the accountability structure is governance theater.

This is the connection to the Bainbridge Warning. Tongue-tie is one of the earliest detectable signs that the capability-governance gap is open. The organization can tell you what the system does. It cannot tell you what holds the system to account. That gap is the Bainbridge zone.

---

## The Martha Application

The Martha framework's Track A -- Relational Intelligence -- opens with the Tongue-Tie Diagnostic as its first module. The diagnostic is not administered as a pass/fail test. It is used to locate the specific layer where the organization's articulable architecture runs out of specificity.

Some organizations have clear operational descriptions but vague intent specifications. The Martha work begins at intent specification.

Some have clear intent specifications but no stopping conditions. The Martha work begins at constitutional constraint design.

Some have all three but unspecified accountability. The Martha work begins at governance constitution.

The diagnostic locates the layer. The cohort builds from there.
